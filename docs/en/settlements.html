Luzhitsy

Luzhitsy is the oldest Votic village, which remains one of the few places where Votes still live compactly, and, therefore, is the cultural center of the people. The village's age is estimated as more than 500 years old.

The name of the village came from the location on the river Luzhitsa. It was first mentioned in scribe roll of Vodskaya (Votic) Pyatina, 1500, as village Luzhitsy by the Ust-Luga gulf in Nikolsky-Toldozhsky churchyard in the territory of Yamburg district.

The village arose as a result of the merging of two adjacent villages: Pesky and Nizhniye Luzhitsy. The current appearance of Luzhitsy is very different even from that of some 70 years ago, not to mention times more ancient.

The center of the village was marked by a chapel. There was a road that ran past the chapel towards the sea, along which cattle were driven to pasture. The street that formed along this road was named Progonny End (Driving End). In other directions, respective "ends" diverged too.

The main cultural site of Luzhitsy today are preserved houses of traditional three-parted type (winter and summer parts, connected by a canopy) with traditional water platbands.

In 1997, the first Votic Museum was established in Luzhitsy. In the Museum, T. Efimova and other villagers collected 70 exhibits and more than 200 photos of the pre-war period. On the basis of the Museum, work was started on collecting ethnographic material, collecting and systematizing information, collected in various archives, including information on the genealogy of Votic families, etc.

Krakolye

Today Krakole, an abolished village in Ust-Luzhsky rural settlement of Kingisepp district, Leningrad oblast, is a part of the village of Ust-Luga.

The village got its name from the Karakolka river, on the eastern bank of which the old part of the village is still located today. When the border of the village crossed the river, the informal names of Jagapura (Votic) and Haanpera (Izhorian) appeared, which translate as "District over the river".

In science it is customary to divide Koonika valta and Harr valta, as ends, inhabited by different peoples – Votes and Izhorians.

The landlords were obliged to bring Russian peasants, according to the reform of Russification, which was actively implemented in the middle of the 18th century. According to this reform, the 1st Russian court had two "Chukhon"-indigenous male souls.

Therefore, it makes sense to talk about changing the language of mixed Votic-Russian landowner population. We could not find any information about any migrations of Izhorians.

In Krakolie there was a wooden Causeway. The river Karakolka was cleaned every year by the residents themselves. There were gardens on both sides of the river, and the bridge over it served as a dance floor for young people in the evenings.

The village is located on the right bank of the Luga river. On the opposite bank there is the center of the settlement – Ust-Luga, with which the village is connected by a bridge. Krakolie and the neighboring village of Luzhitsy are the main districts of Votic settlement.

Pillovo

Pillovo is a village in Kotelsky rural settlement of Kingisepp district of Leningrad region. It was first mentioned in the scribal book of Vodskaya Pyatina in 1500, as the village of Pilola in the Nikolsky Toldozhsky Pogost in Chyudi of Yamsky uyezd.

This area was inhabited since ancient times, so, to the South-West of the village is a settlement of the last quarter of the first thousand A.D. Also particularly noteworthy is the Holy spring of the Pochaev icon of the Blessed Virgin located in Pillovo.

There is a legend related with Pillovo spring, according to which two boys-brothers smashed a bottle of Holy water from the foot of the most Holy mother of God, located on the Pochaev mountain in Malorossiya; and in that place, by the will of the most Holy virgin descended, a stream appeared, the waters of which more than once healed the seriously ill.

Pillovo spring

The spring on a map

In 1742, Pillovo, as well as Volosovo, and a Large Radilovo passed into the possession of count A. G. Razumovsky. Legend has it that on the advice of a groom, the count cured the inflammation by constantly washing them in the waters of a spring.

In gratitude, on the site of the old wooden chapel under a thatched roof, the count put a new chapel in stone. Every year until 1937 from the temple in the village of Kotly through the villages of Pumalitsy, Rannolovo, Pillovo to the chapel there was a procession with a water sacrament which was performed in the stream.

Undovo

Undovo is an ancient Votic village once included into Toldozhsky churchyard of Chud.

The village bore a variety of names: Illo, Iltovy. The names have long existed in parallel, but Illo and Unto are probably the names of old – time settlers.

All the names of the village are known from the Votic old-timers themselves, the texts of which are published in the book by Lauri Kettunen and Lauri Posti "Samples of the Votian language»

The village of Undovo is located on the banks of the Suma river.
